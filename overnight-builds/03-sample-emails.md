**The 3-Step Quick Reset (When Nothing Else Works):**

1. **Pause & Breathe (30 seconds)**

   Once they're calm, try putting them back down *before* they're fully asleep. Drowsy but awake. If they protest, that's okay—stay close, use your voice or a gentle hand on their chest. The goal isn't perfection; it's progress.

This isn't a magic fix. But it *works* more often than white noise, swaddling, or any single "method" because it respects what your baby actually needs: your presence, not a technique.

If your 8-month-old is suddenly fighting sleep, I need you to hear this:

**They're not being difficult. They're learning to be independent.**

Around 8-10 months, babies hit a massive cognitive leap. They start understanding object permanence (you exist even when they can't see you). They start wanting to explore, move, *choose* things. 

And sleep? Sleep is the opposite of all of that.

So when your baby resists sleep, what they're really saying is: *"I want to keep practicing this new skill. I want to stay with you. I want to see what happens next."*

It's not rejection. It's development.

But it's still *hard*. 

Here's what I did that night (and what I teach parents now):

**3 Steps to Navigate Sleep Resistance:**

**1. Acknowledge the leap**  
Say it out loud: "You're learning so much right now. I see you." This isn't for your baby—it's for *you*. It shifts your mindset from "Why is this happening?" to "Ah, this is development."

**2. Shorten the routine (temporarily)**  
Babies at this age have short attention spans. If your bedtime routine is 30 minutes, cut it to 15. Read one book instead of three. Sing one song instead of a full playlist. Less time = less opportunity for resistance.

**3. Stay close, but don't rescue**  
When they stand up in the cot, don't immediately pick them up. Stay nearby. Use your voice ("I'm here"). Give them a chance to figure it out. If they escalate, *then* intervene. But try waiting 30 seconds first. You might be surprised.

These steps don't "fix" sleep resistance overnight. But they help you meet your baby where they are—which is exactly what they need.

One mum I worked with, Emma, said this:

*"I spent 2 weeks thinking my son hated me. Then I reframed it as independence, and everything changed. He still resisted sometimes, but I stopped taking it personally. That alone made me a calmer parent."*

Sleep resistance is a phase. It peaks around 8-10 months, then eases as your baby masters their new skills. 

You're not doing anything wrong.

And that's *hard*. But it's also exactly what's supposed to happen.
